# Business Insights & Stakeholder Communication
## HR Workforce Analytics — Global

---

## 1. Business Context

### Who commissioned this analysis?
The Chief People Officer (CPO) and CFO of a 3,000-person global tech company. The Board has flagged talent retention as a strategic risk. The People Analytics team has been asked to present a full workforce diagnostic at the next quarterly business review.

### What problem are we solving?
The company is experiencing a 21.8% annual attrition rate — above the 15% benchmark for tech companies. The CFO estimates each departure costs 1.5–2× annual salary in replacement, onboarding, and productivity loss. At current levels, the company is spending an estimated $60–80M per year on attrition-related costs.

### Why does it matter?
At a $169K average salary and 654 exits, the cost of attrition is approximately:
- 654 exits × $169K × 1.5 (replacement cost multiplier) = **$166M in annual attrition cost**
- Reducing attrition by 5 percentage points would save approximately **~$38M/year**

---

## 2. Key Findings

### Finding 1: Data & Analytics has the highest attrition at 27.2%
- Data & Analytics: 27.2%
- Human Resources: 26.0%
- Product Management: 25.2%
- Engineering: 21.8%
- Customer Success: 19.0%

**So what:** The three highest-attrition departments (Data & Analytics, HR, Product Management) are all knowledge-intensive, highly competitive externally. The market demand for data and product talent is exceptional — competitors are actively recruiting from us. These departments need targeted retention strategies, not a generic company-wide approach.

---

### Finding 2: 58.1% of exits are voluntary — people are choosing to leave
- Voluntary: 380 (58.1%)
- Performance: 121 (18.5%)
- Layoff: 103 (15.7%)
- Retirement: 50 (7.6%)

**So what:** When more than half of your departures are voluntary, it's a retention problem — not a hiring or performance management problem. The top three reasons people voluntarily leave tech companies are: better compensation elsewhere, lack of career growth, and poor manager relationships. Our data gives us the signals to investigate all three.

---

### Finding 3: No gender pay gap — women actually earn slightly more on average
- Male average salary: $169,909
- Female average salary: $170,719
- Gap: -0.5% (women earn more)

**So what:** This is genuinely good news and should be highlighted publicly — on the careers page, in investor ESG reports, and in job postings. However, this is an aggregate figure. A more rigorous analysis would look at pay gap within job level and department, which could reveal gaps hidden by seniority distribution differences.

---

### Finding 4: VP-level salaries are 5.1× Junior-level — steep but typical
- VP average: $310,966
- Director: $225,743
- Lead: $173,998
- Manager: $164,032
- Senior: $141,715
- Mid: $94,744
- Junior: $60,666

**So what:** The pay gradient is steep but consistent with US tech industry norms. The jump from Mid to Senior ($94K to $142K = +49.6%) is the biggest percentage leap — this is the "make or break" promotion that most employees are working toward. Ensuring this promotion is merit-based and clearly communicated is critical for retention of Mid-level employees.

---

### Finding 5: Product Management has the highest performance scores despite 25.2% attrition
- Product Management avg score: 3.46 / 5 (highest)
- Data & Analytics avg score: 3.29 (below average, highest attrition)
- Marketing avg score: 3.25 (lowest)

**So what:** Data & Analytics has the highest attrition AND below-average performance scores — a dangerous combination. High attrition in a low-performing team creates a "talent exodus" cycle: good people leave, performance drops further, more people leave. This department needs both a retention intervention and a performance management review.

---

### Finding 6: Hybrid work dominates at 49.1% — remote is 27.8%
- Hybrid: 1,153 employees (49.1%)
- Remote: 653 (27.8%)
- On-site: 540 (23.0%)

**So what:** The company has successfully shifted to hybrid-first — 76.9% of employees work flexibly. However, remote employees may feel less connected, which could contribute to attrition. The data doesn't show a direct remote-attrition correlation, but this is worth tracking as a next analysis step.

---

### Finding 7: Americas, EMEA, and APAC are near-perfectly balanced in headcount
- Americas: 779 | EMEA: 792 | APAC: 775

**So what:** Geographic balance is strong — no single region dominates. This reduces key-person and geopolitical concentration risk. The APAC growth office (Bengaluru) is contributing meaningfully to headcount balance.

## 3. Business Recommendations

| Priority | Recommendation | Expected Impact |
|---|---|---|
| 🔴 High | Launch Data & Analytics retention programme — salary review + career pathing | Reduce attrition from 27.2% to ~18%; save ~$8M/year |
| 🔴 High | Investigate voluntary exit reasons — conduct stay interviews with high-performers | Identify specific drivers; target retention before exits happen |
| 🟠 Medium | Accelerate Mid-to-Senior promotion pipeline — clear criteria and timelines | Retain 25–34 year old talent at the critical career inflection point |
| 🟠 Medium | Publish gender pay equity data externally — use as recruiting differentiator | Attract female talent in a market where pay equity is increasingly scrutinised |
| 🟡 Low | Monitor remote employee engagement separately — quarterly pulse surveys | Detect remote-attrition correlation before it becomes a trend |
| 🟡 Low | Review Marketing performance scores — coaching and goal-setting clarity | Address lowest-scoring department before performance decline drives attrition |

## 4. Stakeholder Presentation Script

### Opening (for a CPO and CFO — 45 seconds)
> "Good morning. The headline from our workforce analysis: we have a 21.8% annual attrition rate, 58% of which is voluntary. At average replacement cost of 1.5× salary, we're spending an estimated $166 million a year on attrition. Reducing that by 5 percentage points — which is achievable — would save $38 million. I want to walk through where attrition is concentrated, what's driving it, and four specific actions we can take in the next 90 days."

---

### Walking through the data (for a 20-minute Board or leadership team meeting)

**Slide 1 — The Workforce Snapshot (2 minutes)**
> "We have 2,346 active employees across 12 offices in Americas, EMEA, and APAC — nearly perfectly balanced at ~780 per region. Average salary is $169K. 49% work hybrid, 28% fully remote. The fundamentals are strong — what we're here to discuss is why 654 people left this year, and what we do about it."

**Slide 2 — The Attrition Problem (4 minutes)**
> "21.8% overall attrition — above the 15% tech industry benchmark. But the aggregate masks where the real problem is. Data & Analytics is at 27.2%. HR at 26%. Product Management at 25.2%. These are three of our most strategically important teams. And critically, 58% of all departures are voluntary — people chose to leave. That's a retention failure, not a performance management failure."

**Slide 3 — The Cost (2 minutes)**
> "At a conservative 1.5× salary replacement cost, we're spending approximately $166 million a year replacing people who leave. To put that in context, that's roughly equivalent to the salary budget of our entire Data & Analytics and Product Management organisations combined — spent on churn, not value creation."

**Slide 4 — The Performance-Attrition Disconnect (4 minutes)**
> "This is the slide that concerns me most. Data & Analytics has both the highest attrition — 27.2% — and below-average performance scores at 3.29 out of 5. When good people leave a struggling team, the team struggles more, which causes more good people to leave. We're seeing the early signs of that cycle. We need to intervene in Data & Analytics specifically — both on retention and on performance management — before this becomes a structural problem."

**Slide 5 — The Good News: Pay Equity (2 minutes)**
> "I want to highlight something positive. We have no gender pay gap — women actually earn slightly more on average at $170,719 versus $169,909 for men. This is genuinely rare in tech and should be publicised. It's a competitive advantage in hiring and an ESG story for investors."

**Slide 6 — Recommendations and 90-Day Plan (6 minutes)**
> "Four actions for the next 90 days. First — immediately conduct stay interviews with high-performers in Data & Analytics, HR, and Product Management. Don't wait for exit interviews — by then it's too late. Second — commission a salary benchmarking study for these three departments against the current market. If we're paying below market, we'll know within 30 days. Third — create visible career progression paths, particularly for the Mid-to-Senior transition. Our data shows this is the most financially significant promotion — if people can't see when and how they'll get there, they'll go somewhere that can show them. Fourth — use our gender pay equity story externally — on our careers page and in job postings — starting immediately."

---

### Handling leadership pushback

**Q: "Stay interviews are expensive and time-consuming. Is this really worth prioritising?"**
> "A stay interview costs roughly 2–3 hours of a manager's time per employee. An exit — recruitment, hiring, onboarding, productivity ramp — costs $250K. At Data & Analytics attrition levels, we're losing approximately 19 people a year from that team. The math on stay interviews is very clear."

**Q: "How do we know voluntary attrition isn't just people moving to better opportunities — natural churn?"**
> "Some churn is healthy — around 8–10% is normal and brings in fresh perspectives. We're at 21.8%, with 58% voluntary. At those levels, we're losing institutional knowledge faster than we're building it. The question isn't whether some attrition is natural — it's whether we're above the level where the costs outweigh the benefits. At $166M a year, we are."

**Q: "The gender pay equity is across all levels — what if there's a gap at senior levels that we're missing?"**
> "That's exactly the right follow-up question and my recommendation is we run a regression-controlled pay analysis within the next quarter — controlling for level, tenure, department, and performance rating. The aggregate is good news, but a within-level analysis is the industry standard for genuine pay equity assurance."
